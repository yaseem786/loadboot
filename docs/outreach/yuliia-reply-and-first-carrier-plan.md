**a) She wrote "Hi Roman."** Either your WhatsApp profile name shows something else, or she's mixed you up with another application. Clear it up in the first line — if she thinks she's talking to a different person or company, nothing else you say lands properly.

**b) She asked for "average monthly earnings for someone new." There is no average.** Nobody has ever held this role at LoadBoot. If you invent a number she will believe it, plan around it, and find out in month two. Then she leaves and tells people. Say plainly that she'd be the first, show the math, and give a *range you'd actually stand behind*.

# 2. The first carrier — Ross Rosant (hotshot, Holiday FL)

**His profile:** CDL owner-operator · 2015 Ford F-550 · 40' gooseneck · hotshot / equipment / vehicle / power-only / partial flatbed · FL–GA–AL–TN–SC–NC, open nationwide for the right rate · **no forced dispatch** · wants to be consulted before booking.

## Your worry is the right worry

"If I approve him and can't give him loads, he leaves angry" — yes. That is exactly how small dispatch operations die. He tells three other owner-operators, and in this industry that's the whole reputation.

But the premise underneath it is wrong, and it's worth fixing:

## You do not need your own brokers or shippers

That's not how dispatch works. **The carrier's authority is the relationship.** Ross has his own MC number, his own insurance, his own W-9. A dispatcher books loads *on the carrier's behalf, under the carrier's authority*, from load boards and broker postings. The broker contracts with Ross, pays Ross. You're the person who finds the load, negotiates the rate, handles the paperwork, and takes 5%.

So the question isn't "where are my brokers." It's:

### What you actually need before approving him

1. **A load board subscription.** DAT or Truckstop.com — roughly $150–300/month. This is the real cost of entry and there's no way around it. Without it you cannot source a single load, and approving him would be dishonest.
2. **The signed dispatch agreement.** He hasn't signed it yet — that's the last item on his checklist. It sets the 5%, the no-forced-dispatch terms, and your authority to negotiate for him. Do not book anything before it's signed.
3. **His carrier packet ready to send** — MC certificate, COI, W-9, signed W-9, NOA if he factors. Brokers ask for this before every first load. You already have all of it in the portal, which is your actual advantage: you can send a complete packet in two minutes while other dispatchers scramble.

### Is his freight actually available?

Yes — hotshot and partial flatbed in the FL/GA/AL/TN/SC/NC corridor is one of the more active segments on the boards. A 40' gooseneck behind an F-550 covers equipment hauls, vehicle moves, and partial flatbed, which is a wider net than a plain hotshot. His lane preference is a real market, not a dead one. Rates are the fight, not availability.

## How to approach him — under-promise deliberately

Do not sell him "we'll keep you loaded." Say the opposite. Something like:

> Ross — your file is complete apart from the dispatch agreement. Before you sign it I want to be straight with you about where we are, because I'd rather lose you now than waste your time.
>
> We're early. You'd be one of our first carriers. What that means in practice:
>
> - **You get real attention.** You're not carrier #400 in a queue. I'll work your truck personally.
> - **5% of linehaul, and only when you get paid.** No monthly fee, no setup fee, nothing up front. If I don't find you freight, I don't earn anything either — our incentives point the same direction.
> - **No contract and no forced dispatch**, exactly as you asked. Every load comes to you with the rate and the details before I commit to anything. You can walk away any week, no notice, no penalty.
> - **Here's my honest commitment:** give me two weeks. If I haven't put you on a load you're happy with in that time, you owe me nothing and I'll say so myself.
>
> Your lanes and your equipment are a segment I can work. Sign the agreement when you're ready and I'll start looking the same day.

## Why this handles the "he'll leave angry" risk

He can't feel cheated by something you told him in advance. The anger comes from a promise that wasn't kept — so don't make the promise. A two-week no-obligation window costs you nothing (you earn nothing anyway if you fail) and it makes the offer easy to say yes to.

And if you *do* get him a good load in those two weeks, you've got a reference carrier — the single most valuable thing an early dispatch company can own.

## The discipline rule

**Approve carriers at the speed you can actually serve them.** One carrier well served beats ten neglected. Ross is a good carrier #1: specific, equipped, realistic, and asking for exactly the terms LoadBoot already offers.

Do not let Yuliia onboard twenty more until Ross has been dispatched successfully.
